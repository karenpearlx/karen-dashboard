songwriting playbook

the craft.

techniques for writing songs that stick. learned from taylor swift, applied to your lyrics.

dynamics & structure

Drop Technique

Chorus starts sparser and lower energy than the verse, builds tension, then hits a moment of silence before exploding into a high-energy post-chorus. Creates anticipation. Example: "I Knew You Were Trouble"

S.I.A. (Section Impact Accentuator)

Pull parts or all instrumentation out to spotlight a hook, usually at the end of a chorus. Everything cuts out so the hook lands harder. Example: "Love Story", "Lover"

vocal & lyrical techniques

Vocal Register Contrast

Shift between low and high registers based on what the lyrics need. Low register for negative/sad lyrics, high register for passion/desire. The register matches the emotion. Example: "Cardigan"

Production Mirrors Lyrics

Tiny sounds that make the lyric land. "High heels on cobblestones" sound in Cardigan, pen click in "Blank Space" before "and I'll write your name"

Duets with Countering Lyrics

Two voices singing different sides of the story simultaneously. He sings "you never gave a warning sign" while she sings "i gave so many signs" — listeners hear both perspectives at once. Example: "Exile"

Changing Chorus Lyrics

Most pop songs repeat the exact same chorus. Change the words each time to move the story forward. Over half of Taylor's hits do this. Creates narrative momentum.

signature moments

"Swiftisms"

Signature vocal embellishments — laughs, quips, spoken words, clever hooks spotlighted by silence. "Like, ever" and the laugh in "We Are Never Getting Back Together." These became iconic but she phased them out after Reputation to avoid predictability.

Nonsense Lyrics with Purpose

The "whoa" in "Cruel Summer" isn't random — it rhymes with the hook AND sounds like "woe" which fits the negative emotion. Even filler words serve a purpose.

Elevated vocabulary for lyrics: reckless desire, oblivious to the war, gravity of your gaze, fortress built from sand, paralyzed, undertow, surrender this charade, cascade, paradigm, dissolve, sanctuary, remnants, phantom, silhouette, constellation, unraveling, fractured, illuminate, remnants of what we were

Balance poetic with conversational. Mix complex metaphors with accessible everyday phrasing so the storyline stays easy to grasp.

Emily Dickinson

American poet · Amherst recluse · 1830–1886

jul 22, 2026 ▼

childhood & early life

Emily Elizabeth Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, into a family that took intellect seriously and emotion somewhat suspiciously. Her father, Edward Dickinson, was a lawyer, treasurer of Amherst College, and later a congressman. Her mother, Emily Norcross Dickinson, was quieter and often ill. Emily was the middle child: Austin was older, Lavinia was younger, and the three grew up in a house where duty, reading, religion, and reputation all pressed against the windows.

She was educated more seriously than many girls of her time. She attended Amherst Academy, where she studied literature, botany, geology, Latin, history, and philosophy, then spent less than a year at Mount Holyoke Female Seminary. The great religious revivals of her youth expected young people to publicly declare conversion. Emily resisted. She was not shallowly rebellious; she simply would not hand over her inner life just because the room demanded it. Very Scorpio-coded before Scorpio memes had infrastructure.

Death entered early. Her cousin Sophia Holland died when Emily was a teenager, and the loss disturbed her so deeply that the family sent her away for rest. Amherst itself kept mortality visible: the Dickinson home sat near the cemetery, and funerals passed through ordinary days. That proximity became one of her lifelong obsessions. She did not write about death like a preacher. She wrote about it like someone watching a locked door and wondering what kind of room was behind it.

what she did & why she's remembered

Dickinson wrote nearly 1,800 poems, but only a handful were published while she lived, and those were usually altered to fit normal nineteenth-century taste. Normal taste was not ready for her. Her lines broke grammar open. Her dashes made silence visible. Her slant rhymes sounded like a thought refusing to sit politely in a chair. She wrote about death, God, desire, nature, fame, dread, ecstasy, grief, and the private violence of consciousness.

Between about 1858 and 1865, she entered an astonishing period of productivity, copying poems into small hand-sewn booklets now called fascicles. She was not an accidental recluse scribbling cute verses in a bedroom. She was a disciplined artist building a private architecture for language. She understood that publication was not the only proof of seriousness. Sometimes the work is real before the world knows what to do with it.

Her public image became exaggerated after death: the woman in white, the ghost of Amherst, the spinster hidden upstairs. There is truth in the seclusion, but it is too small a frame. Dickinson had correspondents, intense friendships, literary ambitions, a brutal sense of humor, and an almost surgical ability to cut through polite lies. She was private, not empty. Huge difference.

family life

Emily remained deeply tied to her family home. Her brother Austin lived next door at The Evergreens with his wife, Susan Gilbert Dickinson. Susan mattered enormously: she was Emily's sister-in-law, neighbor, reader, critic, correspondent, and one of the most charged emotional presences in her life. Emily sent Susan hundreds of letters and poems. Some scholars read the relationship as romantic, and whether one names it that way or not, the intimacy was not casual.

Her sister Lavinia, called Vinnie, became the keeper of the house and later the accidental guardian of Emily's immortality. Emily had asked Lavinia to burn her papers after her death, but when Vinnie found the fascicles, she recognized that these were not ordinary scraps. Good for Vinnie. Sometimes disobedience saves literature.

The family was loving but not simple. Edward was stern and protective. Her mother was emotionally distant in ways Emily noticed. Austin's later affair with Mabel Loomis Todd tore through the family and complicated the publication of Emily's poems after death, because Todd became one of the first editors of the work. Imagine your literary afterlife being handled partly by the woman who helped wreck family dinner. Extremely Victorian. Also a mess.

romantic life

Dickinson never married, which made later readers assume she either had no romantic life or a secret one. The truth is more interesting and less convenient. Her poems are full of erotic voltage, longing, refusal, devotion, and power. She knew desire. She just did not package it into the life script people expected.

There were men around the mystery. Benjamin Newton, a young law student in her father's office, encouraged her reading and gave her Emerson. Reverend Charles Wadsworth became an important spiritual and emotional figure; after his death, she called him phrases like "my Philadelphia" and "my Shepherd." In later life she had a warm, possibly romantic attachment to Judge Otis Lord, a widower and family friend. They exchanged affectionate letters, and marriage may even have been considered, but he died in 1884.

Then there is Susan. Emily wrote to her with intensity that still startles: admiring, wounded, playful, jealous, devotional. Some editors softened or obscured that relationship for decades. Dickinson's love life, like her poems, resists being pinned down cleanly. She was drawn to minds, absences, distance, and impossible rooms. Naturally, because apparently nobody brilliant in literature knows how to choose emotionally available people. meow.

life until death

As Emily aged, she withdrew more from public life. By her thirties and forties, she was often seen only by family and close visitors, sometimes speaking to guests from behind a door or sending down notes and flowers. But withdrawal did not mean disappearance. She gardened intensely, baked, wrote letters, watched the town, and kept producing poems that made ordinary objects feel radioactive.

The 1880s brought losses. Her parents died. Her nephew Gilbert, Austin and Susan's young son, died in 1883, devastating her. Judge Otis Lord died in 1884. Emily's own health declined, likely from Bright's disease, a kidney illness. Her last known letter to her cousins Louise and Frances Norcross was only two words: "Called Back." She died on May 15, 1886, at fifty-five.

After the funeral, Lavinia found the poems. The first volume appeared in 1890, edited heavily to make Dickinson look less strange. Later editions restored more of her punctuation, variants, and force. The world needed time to catch up with the woman upstairs. Annoying, but predictable.

their works

Dickinson is best understood by reading her directly. The voice is compressed, sharp, and weirdly modern. She can make hope sound like a bird, grief like weather, death like a carriage ride, and success like something only the defeated understand.

Mary Shelley

gothic novelist · author of Frankenstein · 1797–1851

jul 21, 2026 ▼

childhood & early life

Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin was born in London on August 30, 1797, into a house already crowded with dangerous ideas. Her mother was Mary Wollstonecraft, author of A Vindication of the Rights of Woman. Her father was William Godwin, a political philosopher and novelist. Mary inherited brilliance from both sides, but inheritance came with a wound: her mother died eleven days after giving birth to her.

So Mary grew up with a dead mother who was everywhere: in books, in portraits, in reputation, and in the graveyard where Mary liked to read. Godwin educated her informally, letting her use his library and listen to the radicals and writers who visited. At ten, she published a comic poem, Mounseer Nongtongpaw. Annoying child genius behavior, but fine, she earned it.

Her childhood was not gentle. Godwin remarried Mary Jane Clairmont in 1801, and Mary never bonded easily with her stepmother. The house mixed intellectual freedom with emotional scarcity and money pressure. That tension matters because Frankenstein is full of abandoned children, failed parents, brilliant men who create life and then refuse responsibility, and creatures who become monstrous after being unloved.

what she did & why she's remembered

Mary Shelley is remembered above all for writing Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus, published anonymously in 1818 when she was twenty. It is often called the first major science fiction novel, but that label is too tidy. It is gothic horror, philosophical argument, family tragedy, creation myth, grief engine, and revenge story. It asks what happens when intelligence outruns tenderness.

The origin story came from the rainy summer of 1816 near Lake Geneva, when Mary, Percy Bysshe Shelley, Lord Byron, Claire Clairmont, and John Polidori were trapped indoors by bad weather. Byron proposed that everyone write a ghost story. After listening to conversations about galvanism and reanimation, Mary had a waking dream of a student kneeling beside the thing he had assembled, watching it stir with life.

But she was not a one-book accident. She wrote Valperga, The Last Man, The Fortunes of Perkin Warbeck, Lodore, and Falkner. She also wrote travel books, short stories, essays, and biographies, and edited Percy Shelley's posthumous poems. Her life after Percy's death was writing, editing, raising her son, protecting reputations, and staying financially alive.

family life

Mary's family was a Romantic-era disaster with excellent footnotes. Her half-sister was Fanny Imlay. Her stepsister Claire Clairmont became tied to the Shelley-Byron circle. Mary and Percy had four children. Their first daughter lived only a few days. Mary wrote in her journal that she dreamed the baby came back to life after being warmed by the fire, which sits behind Frankenstein like a small ghost.

Their daughter Clara died in infancy. Their son William, nicknamed Willmouse, died of malaria in 1819. Only Percy Florence Shelley survived into adulthood. Mary also lost Fanny Imlay, who died by suicide in 1816, and Harriet Westbrook, Percy's first wife, who died by suicide later that year. She was surrounded by death before she was twenty.

romantic life

Mary met Percy Bysshe Shelley as a teenager. He was brilliant, intense, radical, already married, and exactly the kind of man who should have come with a warning label. In 1814, when Mary was sixteen, she ran away with him to Europe, taking Claire Clairmont with them. Her father was furious, society judged her, and Percy was still married to Harriet.

They married in December 1816 after Harriet's death. The marriage was intellectually electric but emotionally brutal: money trouble, jealousy, illness, dead children, constant movement, and Percy's complicated ideas about love. Mary loved him deeply, but after he drowned in 1822, she also had to survive him. The gothic heroine became a widow at twenty-four, then a mother, editor, professional writer, and keeper of memory. Extremely exhausting. Very operations lead of her.

life until death

After Percy's death, Mary returned to England with her surviving son. Percy's father controlled money tightly and did not want her publishing a biography of Percy. Mary had to balance grief, reputation, motherhood, and poverty with almost insulting calm. She published Percy's Posthumous Poems in 1824, but pressure forced her to withdraw it. Later she edited editions of his poetry and prose, shaping his legacy.

She kept writing even when critics were unfair. The Last Man, her plague apocalypse, was poorly received in its time and feels much more modern now. Mary died in London on February 1, 1851, at fifty-three, after years of illness, possibly a brain tumor. After her death, her family reportedly found relics of Percy in her desk, including locks of hair and what were believed to be remains of his heart. Romanticism was grief with better stationery.

their works

The best way to understand Mary Shelley is to hear the pressure inside her own sentences. She writes ambition beautifully, then punishes it. She writes loneliness so precisely it gets embarrassing. She understood that the monster is not ugly because he is stitched together. He becomes frightening because nobody can bear to love what they made.

Hypatia of Alexandria

mathematician · philosopher · astronomer · c. 355–415 CE

jul 20, 2026 ▼

childhood & early life

Hypatia was born in Alexandria, Egypt, sometime around 355 CE, though some sources place her closer to 370. Alexandria was no longer the shining Hellenistic capital it had been under the Ptolemies, but it was still one of the brain-centers of the late Roman world: Greek philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, Christian theology, pagan ritual, imperial politics, and street violence all crowded into the same city.

Her father was Theon of Alexandria, a mathematician and astronomer connected with the old scholarly tradition of the Alexandrian Museum. He edited Euclid's Elements and worked on astronomical texts. That matters because Hypatia did not emerge from nowhere. She was raised inside a house where geometry, star charts, instruments, and commentary work were ordinary daily life.

There are later legends that Theon trained her body as rigorously as her mind, making her row, run, and ride so she would not become a fragile scholar. Those details are difficult to verify, but the shape of the story feels right: Hypatia became famous not just for being learned, but for self-command. Ancient sources describe her as publicly confident, disciplined, and unusually free in a city where women were not expected to lecture men on Plato and mathematics.

what she did & why she's remembered

Hypatia became the head of a Neoplatonic school in Alexandria. Neoplatonism was not just abstract philosophy; it was a whole way of ordering reality. The visible world was treated as a shadow of higher forms of truth, and mathematics was one of the ladders upward. To understand geometry or astronomy was, in that worldview, to train the soul to recognize order.

She taught philosophy, mathematics, and astronomy to elite students, including Christians. Her most famous student was Synesius of Cyrene, who later became a bishop. His surviving letters show the kind of authority she had. He writes to her not like a man politely humoring a woman teacher, but like someone appealing to the person whose judgment still outranked everyone else's.

Her technical work seems to have been commentary and teaching rather than flashy invention. She is associated with commentaries on Diophantus's Arithmetica, Apollonius's Conics, and astronomical tables connected with Ptolemy. That sounds dry until you remember what commentary meant in late antiquity: preserving, explaining, correcting, and transmitting difficult works so future students could still use them. She was basically maintaining the source code of ancient mathematics while the operating system of the empire was crashing. Extremely Karen-coded, actually.

She is also linked through Synesius with scientific instruments. One letter asks her to have a hydroscope, probably a hydrometer for measuring liquid density, made for him. Another refers to an astrolabe, an instrument used to model the heavens. Whether she invented these devices is debated, but the letters make clear that her expertise was practical, not just theoretical.

family life

The only family member we can speak about with confidence is her father, Theon. He appears to have educated her seriously and may have collaborated with her. Some scholars think Hypatia helped edit or continue parts of Theon's mathematical and astronomical work, though it is difficult to separate their hands because ancient manuscripts rarely behave nicely for modern historians.

There is no reliable evidence that she had children. Her school seems to have functioned almost like an intellectual household: students wrote to her, asked for recommendations, asked for help, sent greetings, and treated her as a central authority figure long after leaving Alexandria. Synesius called her his mother, sister, teacher, and benefactress in one letter, which is dramatic, but Greeks were dramatic. Still, it tells us the emotional structure of her circle. She was not merely a lecturer. She was a point of orientation.

romantic life

Ancient sources present Hypatia as unmarried and celibate. Damascius says she remained a virgin, and later stories exaggerate this into almost mythic purity. One famous anecdote claims that when a student fell in love with her, she confronted him with the physical reality of desire rather than encouraging romantic fantasy. The story may be hostile gossip, symbolic legend, or philosophical theater, but its point is clear: Hypatia was remembered as someone who refused to be reduced to an object of longing.

That does not mean she was cold. The letters from her students suggest intense devotion around her. But her public identity was built on intellect, restraint, and autonomy. In a world that preferred women silent, married, and contained, she walked through Alexandria in a philosopher's cloak, taught men in public, and advised political leaders. She made herself socially legible as a philosopher first. That was the armor.

life until death

By the early 400s, Alexandria was politically poisonous. The old pagan intellectual world was shrinking. Christianity was now powerful, but not peaceful; factions fought over doctrine, authority, and control of the city. Hypatia was respected by the Roman prefect Orestes, who was in conflict with Cyril, the bishop of Alexandria. That friendship made her useful as a rumor target.

In 415 CE, a mob led by a church reader named Peter attacked her carriage. Socrates Scholasticus, a Christian historian writing not long after, says they dragged her into a church, killed her, dismembered her body, and burned the remains. He does not frame her death as a clean pagan-versus-Christian morality play. He says she became a victim of political jealousy. That is uglier, honestly. She was murdered because a powerful woman with influence became inconvenient to men fighting over power.

Her death became symbolic far beyond the facts. Later writers turned her into a martyr for science, a pagan saint, a feminist icon, a warning about fanaticism, or a casualty of collapsing civilization. Some versions are too neat. The real Hypatia was sharper than the symbol: a teacher, editor, mathematician, philosopher, and civic figure who survived by discipline until politics made discipline irrelevant.

quotes & surviving words

Important caveat: the famous lines usually attributed to Hypatia, like "reserve your right to think" and "all formal dogmatic religions are fallacious," are not authenticated ancient quotes. They come from much later fictionalized writing. Her own works do not survive as personal prose, so the honest version is that we mostly have words written about her and letters written to her.

works associated with her

Her own books are lost, but ancient and later sources associate her with a commentary on Diophantus's Arithmetica, a commentary on Apollonius's Conics, and work on astronomical tables connected with Ptolemy. These were not diary-like writings, so there are no poetic passages to quote like Sappho. Hypatia's surviving legacy is more severe: equations, instruments, edited texts, students, and the terrifying fact that a mind can become politically dangerous.

Sappho

ancient greek poet · the tenth muse · c. 630–570 BCE

jul 19, 2026 ▼

childhood & early life

She was born sometime around 630 BCE on the island of Lesbos, in a town called Eresos—though she would spend most of her life in Mytilene, the island's largest city. Her family was aristocratic and wealthy, the kind of family that could afford to give a daughter an education in music and poetry, which was rare for women in ancient Greece.

She grew up with at least two brothers: Charaxus, the eldest, who would later become a wine merchant and cause the family some embarrassment; and Larichos, the youngest, who was said to be so beautiful he served wine at civic gatherings in Mytilene—a position of honor reserved for the most handsome young men of noble families. Some sources mention a third brother, Eurygios, but little is known of him.

Her father's name is uncertain—ancient sources give different names—and he may have died when she was young. What we do know is that Sappho grew up during a time of political upheaval. Lesbos was caught between tyrants and aristocratic factions fighting for control, and at some point in her youth or early adulthood, Sappho was exiled. She fled to Sicily, likely to the city of Syracuse, and would remain there for years before eventually returning home.

what she did & why she's remembered

Back in Mytilene, Sappho became the center of a thiasos—a close-knit community of young women who gathered to study poetry, music, dance, and the worship of Aphrodite and the Muses. Think of it as part school, part salon, part religious sisterhood. Girls from aristocratic families across Greece were sent to her, and under her guidance, they learned to compose and perform lyric poetry—songs meant to be sung to the accompaniment of the lyre.

Sappho wrote in the Aeolic dialect of Greek, and she invented or perfected what we now call the "Sapphic stanza"—a specific meter that would influence poets for millennia. She composed wedding songs, hymns to the gods, and deeply personal poems about desire, jealousy, beauty, and loss. Her voice was immediate and raw in a way that still feels modern. She wrote about watching a woman she loved talking to a man, and feeling her heart race, her tongue go numb, her skin burn. She wrote about longing for someone far away, about the pain of being left behind.

In her own time and for centuries after, she was considered one of the greatest poets who ever lived. Plato called her "the Tenth Muse." Solon, the great Athenian lawmaker, reportedly heard one of her songs at a dinner party and immediately demanded to be taught it, saying he wanted to learn it before he died. The Romans adored her. Catullus translated her. Horace imitated her.

She wrote nine volumes of poetry—thousands of lines. And almost all of it is gone.

Only one complete poem survives: her Ode to Aphrodite, a prayer to the goddess of love asking for help in winning back someone who has rejected her. The rest exists only in fragments—lines quoted by other ancient authors who admired her, scraps of papyrus pulled from the Egyptian desert, torn and incomplete. Some fragments are just a few words. Some are haunting half-sentences that cut off mid-thought. We have maybe 650 lines total, out of what may have been 10,000 or more.

family life

Sappho had a daughter named Cleis, whom she loved fiercely. In one fragment, she writes: "I have a daughter, golden like a flower, my beloved Cleis—I would not trade her for all of Lydia or lovely Lesbos." The identity of Cleis's father is unknown.

Some ancient sources claim she was married to a wealthy man named Cercylas from the island of Andros. But many scholars believe this was a crude joke invented by later comic poets—the name "Cercylas" sounds like a Greek word for male genitalia, and "Andros" means "man." It was the ancient equivalent of saying she was married to "Dick from Man Island."

She also wrote about her brothers. Charaxus, the wine merchant, fell in love with an expensive courtesan named Rhodopis in Egypt and spent a fortune to buy her freedom. Sappho was not pleased. A poem recently discovered in 2014—the "Brothers Poem"—shows her worrying about Charaxus's return from a sea voyage and mentioning Larichos, perhaps hoping he would step up and restore the family's honor.

romantic life

Her poetry makes clear that she loved women—passionately, physically, with the full intensity of desire and devotion. She wrote of watching a woman laugh and feeling her heart shake in her chest. She wrote of longing for someone who had left, of jealousy when she saw a rival, of the ache of absence. Her poems mention several women by name: Anactoria, Atthis, Gongyla, Mnasidica. Some may have been students in her thiasos who eventually left to marry. Others may have been lovers. The lines between teacher, mentor, friend, and beloved were fluid in her world.

The words "lesbian" and "sapphic" come from her. For centuries, people tried to explain away or minimize her desire for women. Victorian translators changed pronouns. Scholars invented a "second Sappho" who was a prostitute to explain away the erotic content. But her words are clear enough. She wrote about women the way men wrote about women, and she did not apologize for it.

There is also the legend of Phaon—a young, beautiful ferryman whom Aphrodite blessed with irresistible beauty. The story goes that Sappho fell madly in love with him, and when he rejected her, she threw herself from the Leucadian cliffs into the sea. It's a dramatic story, and it's almost certainly fiction—invented centuries after her death, probably by comic playwrights who wanted to "straighten" her legacy or punish her for her desires. Modern scholars dismiss it entirely.

life until death

She lived into old age—unusual for someone whose life story was later dramatized with a tragic suicide. Several of her surviving fragments mention aging with a kind of wistful acceptance. She writes about her hair turning white, her knees no longer able to carry her in the dance, her body "seized by old age." But she does not rage against it. There's a quietness to these poems, a sense of someone who has lived fully and is watching herself slow down.

We don't know exactly when she died, or how. Probably sometime around 570 BCE, in her sixties or seventies, in Mytilene, surrounded by the sea and the women she had taught and loved. No grand death. No cliffs. Just an ending, like the ends of her poems that have been torn away by time.

Her poetry was still read and celebrated for centuries after her death. The library of Alexandria held all nine volumes of her work. But as the ancient world fell apart—as Rome declined, as Christianity rose, as libraries burned and manuscripts were lost—her words began to disappear. By the 8th or 9th century CE, she existed only in quotations. What we have now is what survived by accident: papyrus scraps used to wrap mummies, quotes in grammatical textbooks, fragments preserved because someone else mentioned them in passing.

And yet she was right about one thing. In one of her fragments, she wrote: "The Muses have filled my life with delight. And when I die I shall not be forgotten."

She wasn't.
